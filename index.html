The Importance of Supply Chains for Artificial Intelligence

Unlocking the Future: How Data Trade is Set to Outpace Global Trade Growth

January, 2025

As we approach 2024, global trade is projected to surge to an astonishing $32 trillion. In stark contrast, data trade—the business of data brokering—remains relatively modest, accounting for less than 1% of this figure at approximately $262 billion. Yet, the trajectory of data trade is remarkable, with a forecasted growth rate of 14% by 2025, far outpacing the 3% growth expected in global trade. This accelerating growth is driven by the increasing value of data in our digital economy, particularly as artificial intelligence continues to rise.

Supply chains, both tangible and digital, are the lifeblood of our global economy. However, without transparency, they can become fertile ground for exploitation. Consider this: a T-shirt may embark on an 18,000-kilometer journey before it reaches a European store. An astonishing 80% of worldwide trade relies on these supply chains, providing livelihoods for over 450 million individuals [Source]. Alarmingly, among these workers, around 79 million children are trapped in exploitative labor conditions. The wages earned by garment workers represent a mere 0.6% of the cost of your designer T-shirt. This is not just an issue; it's a crisis that demands collective action from governments, corporations, and consumers alike. Notably, the German Supply Chain Law is a groundbreaking initiative aimed at enhancing transparency in physical supply chains and curbing human rights violations, having been adopted by the German parliament in 2023 [Source].

Now, let’s shift our focus to the digital landscape, which feels very much like the Wild West. Companies facing strict data laws at home often seek refuge in countries with looser regulations, such as India, Mexico, or Egypt. Here’s the process: data collected in these regions is used to support the domestic user bases. This strategy hinges on a powerful concept known as transfer learning. Transfer learning is a vital mechanism in AI that underscores the urgent need for robust data regulations within digital supply chains. It allows AI to leverage knowledge from one domain and apply it to another. For example, insights gained from lung cancer research can be utilized to identify prostate cancer [paper]. This principle extends beyond medical applications, crossing cultural boundaries. This means companies can apply data on prostate cancer from countries like India to analyze cases involving German patients as well as those with lung cancer. However, this raises ethical questions. When personal healthcare data is collected in developing countries—where costs are low and regulations are minimal—it often serves patients in more regulated environments like the West. In regions with weak data regulations, data trading can quickly become exploitative, creating a business model that thrives on fast, cheap data collection and limited oversight.

To navigate the complexities and ethical challenges of digital supply chains, we can leverage innovative tools. One such tool is the model cards initiated by Margaret Mitchell from Hugging Face. These cards function as a digital passport for AI models, detailing their origins, performance, and applications [Source]. This transparency is vital for understanding the capabilities and limitations of AI models that can help mitigate data misuse. By adopting model cards, we can ensure that the data fueling AI is sourced responsibly and the AI built ethically, aligning with principles similar to fair trade in the physical realm. As we move toward an increasingly data-driven future, the lessons learned from traditional supply chains can guide our approach to the emerging data trade. The same diligence that tracks a T-shirt's global journey should be mirrored in the flow of data through the digital landscape. Imagine a future where we can track our personal data with the same precision we use for our packages—“Oh look, my browsing history just made a pit stop in Palo Alto!”

In conclusion, as we embrace the digital age, prioritizing transparency and ethical standards in both tangible and digital supply chains is essential. Only then can we create a future where the benefits of AI are shared equitably among all.
